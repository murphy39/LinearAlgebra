Overview

This course in linear algebra is aimed at beginning graduate students in mathematics. It is also appropriate for advanced undergraduate math majors, as well as graduate students in engineering and computer science. The focus is on finite dimensional vector spaces from a more general point of view than one finds in an undergraduate course. Topics covered include the following (from the catalog): vector spaces, linear transformations, dual spaces, decompositions of spaces, canonical forms. A more detailed list of topics appears below.

Textbook

The main textbook for the course will be either Golan or Lax. (The final choice will be made very soon.)
The Linear Algebra a Beginning Graduate Student Ought to Know, Jonathan Golan.
Linear Algebra and Its Applications, Peter Lax.

The following books are not required, but they might be helpful:
Finite Dimensional Vector Spaces, Paul Halmos.
Matrix Analysis, Horn and Johnson.
Matrix Computations, Golub and Van Loan.
Advanced Linear Algebra, Steven Roman.

Homework

Solving problems is the best way to learn this material. A list of problems, both from the textbook and other sources, will be distributed and updated as the course progresses. Students will solve and submit as many solutions as possible, and 55% of the grade will be based on the number of correct, well written solutions submitted.

Exams

There will be one midterm exam worth 20% of the grade. It will be scheduled as we progress, and notice of at least one week will be given prior to the date of a test. A final exam at the end of the semester will be worth 25% of the grade.

Computing

No prior programming experience is required, and the computing component of the class is not graded. This is a graduate math course, so it will be taught at a relatively high level of abstraction and generality. However, one of the best ways to develop a deeper understanding of the material is to use the computer to experiment with and apply the methods. For this we will use one or two different programming languages, depending in part on student backgrounds and interests, most likely including Matlab/Octave, Sage/Python, or C/LAPACK.

Topics

  1. Fields, vector spaces and algebras over a field, isomorphism theorems.
  2. Linear independence, basis and dimension.
  3. Linear transformations.
  4. The endomorphism algebra of a vector space.
  5. Representation of linear transformations by matrices.
  6. Lattice of subspaces and invariant subspaces.
  7. Determinant and trace.
  8. Spectral theory: eigenvalues, eigenvectors.
  9. Euclidean structure: orthonormal basis, norms, spectral radius.
  10. Normed linear spaces and duality.
  11. Spectral theory of self-adjoint mappings.

Other topics (depending on time and student interest):

  • Krylov subspaces.
  • Self-adjoint endomorphisms.
  • Unitary and normal endomorphisms.
  • Moore-Penrose pseudoinverses.
  • Bilinear transformations and forms.
  • Matrix inequalities and decompositions.
  • Algebras of linear transformations, simultaneous triangularization.
